# First Challenge

## The challenge

Own the testing-ownership problem end to end and force it into an operational pilot.

More concretely: within the next 4 to 6 weeks, lead a cross-functional effort that produces a clear, usable answer to this question:

> Who owns which testing activities, in which environment, for which type of change, and how does work flow from SC1DEV to SC1TEST to client environments?

Then turn that answer into one real pilot, not just a document.

## Why this is the right stretch

This challenge sits at the intersection of the strongest themes in the notes:

- unclear ownership and duplicated responsibility in testing
- testing happening late and as an afterthought
- need for a unified strategy across teams
- weak flow discipline and real-time prioritization
- tension between clear ownership and required collaboration

It is a good stretch because:

- you will have to ask people to commit to boundaries they may prefer to leave vague
- you will need to facilitate disagreement between teams with different incentives
- you will have to turn a strategy conversation into explicit operating rules
- you will need to decide when clarity is good enough instead of waiting for full certainty
- you will have to rely more on influence than on expertise

It is still a reasonable challenge because:

- you already have the context, the stakeholder map, and the evidence
- the topic is already active, so this is not an artificial side quest
- a pilot keeps the scope bounded and practical

## Why this one over the alternatives

Three possible stretch challenges emerged from the notes:

1. Lead the cVation transition plan.
2. Install real Kanban discipline in Swiss Knives.
3. Resolve testing ownership through a live pilot.

The third is the best fit.

- The cVation transition is important, but it is more politically constrained and depends heavily on staffing, budget, and timing above your level.
- Kanban discipline matters, but it is more inward-facing and does not stretch your cross-functional influence as much.
- The testing-ownership challenge forces you to work across ambiguity, conflicting incentives, unclear boundaries, and partial authority.

That is a strong management-growth problem.

## The mission

Produce a simple ownership model for testing, align the critical stakeholders around it, run one pilot, and measure whether it reduced confusion, handoffs, or delay.

The output should be only three things:

1. A one-page ownership matrix.
2. A one-page pilot definition.
3. A short review after the pilot with lessons and next changes.

## What the ownership matrix should answer

- what gets tested in SC1DEV
- what gets tested in SC1TEST
- what remains client-specific
- who defines scenarios
- who automates
- who executes
- who signs off
- what gets escalated where

## What the pilot should look like

Choose one concrete slice, for example:

- one onboarding case such as INTECH or Lindsell Train
- one product or domain slice with meaningful E2E ambiguity
- one environment path from standard to client-facing execution

The goal is not to redesign the whole organization. The goal is to prove one workable model.

## Success criteria

A good result is not that everyone agrees with everything.

A good result is:

- fewer open questions about ownership
- fewer handoffs with lost context
- one pilot that actually ran
- a visible artifact others can reuse
- at least one hard tradeoff made explicit

Useful measures:

- number of ownership questions still unresolved after alignment
- number of teams involved before versus after the pilot
- number of handoffs in the tested flow
- turnaround time for a test-plan or execution decision
- stakeholder feedback on clarity

## What you will learn

If you do this well, you will practice the management muscles that your notes point to most clearly:

- framing ambiguity into decisions
- leading without direct authority
- converting strategy into an operating mechanism
- balancing collaboration with accountability
- making progress under incomplete information

This is a stronger stretch than simply learning more management. It is management.

## Suggested timeline

### Week 1

- define the question narrowly
- write the first draft of the ownership matrix yourself
- keep it concrete, even if imperfect

### Week 2

- review it with Driss first, then 2 to 4 critical stakeholders
- ask one recurring question: "Where would this break in real work?"

### Week 3

- freeze a pilot version
- choose one real case and one small set of success metrics
- do not wait for organization-wide perfection

### Week 4 to 6

- run the pilot
- capture friction, exceptions, and role confusion
- end with a short readout and recommendation

## Guardrails

- do not try to solve all testing at once
- do not aim for a reorganization disguised as a process change
- do not confuse stakeholder politeness with real commitment
- do not let the document become the outcome; the pilot is the outcome

## Where you will probably struggle

Based on the notes, the likely traps are:

- over-respecting existing ambiguity because it feels politically safer
- trying to include everyone before making the draft sharp enough
- letting shared ownership remain vague
- turning the work into a strategy paper instead of an operating agreement

## Core challenge statement

Write the first imperfect ownership model yourself, expose it to disagreement, and still get a pilot running.
